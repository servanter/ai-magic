## What Causes Old Photos to Degrade?

Understanding the causes of photo degradation helps you understand what AI restoration is actually fixing:

- **Fading and color shift**: Silver-based photographs lose density over time; color dye processes shift toward red or yellow as certain dyes degrade faster than others.
- **Physical damage**: Scratches, creases, tears, and fold lines physically remove or displace the photographic medium.
- **Water damage**: Moisture causes emulsion to lift, smear, and develop fungal stains that obscure image detail.
- **Yellowing**: Both paper bases and photographic surfaces yellow with age due to oxidation and environmental pollutants.
- **Dust and surface contamination**: Accumulated dust and handling marks create noise across the image.
- **Silver mirroring**: A common form of deterioration in old prints where the surface develops a bluish-silver metallic sheen.
- **Low resolution of the original**: Many old prints were small in format, so scanned versions lack the pixel density of modern photographs.

## How AI Photo Restoration Works

Modern AI restoration models are trained on large datasets of paired damaged and undamaged images, learning to recognize and reverse specific types of deterioration.

The restoration process typically involves several concurrent operations:

### Noise and Artifact Removal
The AI identifies random noise, dust spots, and film grain and smooths them while preserving genuine image detail. This is one of the areas where AI excels — distinguishing between meaningful detail and random artifacts.

### Scratch and Damage Inpainting
Where physical damage has removed image information entirely (tears, scratches, missing areas), the AI uses context from the surrounding image to intelligently fill in what was likely there. For portraits, this is particularly impressive — the AI can reconstruct partially obscured facial features based on symmetry and surrounding context.

### Contrast and Tonal Restoration
Faded images lose the density in their shadows and highlights. AI restores the tonal range, bringing back contrast and revealing detail that was hidden in the fade.

### Color Restoration
For black-and-white images, some AI tools can add plausible colorization. For faded color photographs, AI can partially reverse color shifts — restoring cooler tones that have yellowed or bringing back the original saturation.

### Super-Resolution
Many old photographs, when scanned, are inherently low-resolution. AI upscaling (super-resolution) can enhance the image to modern display and print standards while adding realistic detail rather than simply blurring the image up.

## What AI Can and Can't Fix

### AI Restoration Works Well For:
- Removing dust, spots, and surface contamination
- Lightening and evening out yellowing
- Improving faded contrast and tonal range
- Filling in small scratches and minor tears
- Removing mild water stains
- Improving sharpness and reducing film grain
- Upscaling to higher resolution
- Partial colorization of black-and-white photos

### AI Restoration Has Limitations For:
- Large areas of completely missing image data (major tears, large water damage)
- Heavily burned or chemically stained regions
- Images that have physically disintegrated or become permanently stuck together
- Reconstructing detail in areas where no information whatsoever remains

For severely damaged images, AI restoration may produce plausible but not historically accurate reconstructions — the AI infers what "should" be there based on visual patterns, but can't know what actually was.

## Best Practices for Photo Scanning and Preservation

AI restoration can only work with what you give it. Here's how to get the best possible input:

### Scanning Tips
- **Use a flatbed scanner** rather than a phone camera when possible — scanners provide more even lighting and better sharpness.
- **Scan at 600 DPI minimum** for standard prints; use 1200 DPI for small (wallet-sized) photographs.
- **Clean your scanner glass** before each scanning session to avoid adding new dust to the digital capture.
- **Handle photos by the edges** to avoid transferring oils from your fingers.
- **Don't force damaged prints flat** if they're curved or brittle — gentle, careful placement is better than risking further damage.

### If Using a Phone Camera
- Place the photo on a clean, evenly lit surface
- Shoot directly above the print, not at an angle
- Use natural, diffused daylight rather than artificial lighting
- Ensure your phone is perfectly steady (use a tripod or brace against a table)
- Take multiple shots and choose the sharpest one

## Preserving Restored Images for the Future

Once you've restored your images, consider these steps for long-term preservation:

- **Store in multiple locations**: External hard drive, cloud storage, and a physical backup.
- **Use lossless file formats**: TIFF or PNG preserve all quality information; avoid JPEG for archival copies.
- **Create a family archive**: Organize images with names, dates, and descriptions so the context isn't lost with future generations.
- **Share with family members**: Distributing copies ensures the images survive even if one copy is lost.
- **Print high-quality reproductions**: A professionally printed reproduction on archival paper can last 100+ years if stored correctly.
